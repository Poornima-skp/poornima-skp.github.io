Case Study

From one question to a running system

Over several months, an ad hoc question to an AI assistant turned into a daily, automated decision-support tool — through the same loop I use for any AI-assisted work: ask, evaluate critically, catch what's wrong, refine, and only then scale it up.

01 — Start narrow
Asked a single, constrained question
Instead of a generic prompt, I set an explicit constraint up front, which shaped every answer that followed.
"I do not want to own this stock. I am just trying to make money by writing puts and letting it expire."
02 — Catch the conflict
Evaluated the answer against my own constraint
The AI surfaced a high-premium trade — but high premium was a signal to interrogate, not a reason to act. I read past the headline number to the actual risk, and rejected the trade because it broke my own stated rule.
03 — Generalize
Turned one answer into a reusable framework
Instead of asking one-off questions stock by stock, I specified explicit, repeatable screening criteria — delta range, volatility threshold, price band — so any candidate could be evaluated the same way.
04 — Correct my own spec
Caught and fixed my own mistake
I reviewed the output against what I actually wanted and corrected a contradiction I'd introduced myself.
"My bad. I do NOT want to trade in stocks that are more than $80."
05 — Automate
Converted the framework into a recurring system
Once the criteria were reliable, I moved from asking on demand to a standing daily report — generated automatically, before markets open, against a consistent rubric I designed.
06 — Iterate on the system itself
Identified a gap and closed it
I noticed Monday reports were missing weekend context that mattered for decision-making, and specified a fix — a structural improvement to the tool, not just a one-off answer.
Why this matters beyond investing: the domain here is personal market research, but the pattern is the transferable part — set explicit constraints, evaluate output rather than trusting it, generalize a good answer into a reusable tool, catch your own errors, then automate and keep refining. That's the same loop I'd bring to building a tool, agent, or evaluation harness for a host organization.
Prompt Refinement

Editing until it's actually right

A separate project — designing a family invitation with AI-generated imagery — shows the same instinct at a smaller, more precise scale: I don't accept a "close enough" output, I specify exactly what's wrong and exactly what should change.

EXAMPLE 1 — Tone
First draft wording
"Please join us for [Name]'s Half Saree Ceremony"
Refinement given
"Instead of 'please join us for' write 'cordially invite for'"
Specified the exact phrase and the exact replacement — not "make it more formal."
EXAMPLE 2 — Voice & ownership
First draft wording
"...their granddaughter and daughter"
Refinement given
"Instead of 'their granddaughter and daughter' write 'our granddaughter and daughter'"
Caught a subtle point-of-view error the model missed — the family should speak in first person on their own invitation.
EXAMPLE 3 — Quantified feedback
First draft result
Sparkle animation applied at full intensity — visually loud, competing with the invitation text.
Refinement given
"I like the flower petals but the sparkle is a bit too much" → specified "about 70% less than current," kept the petals, cut the sparkle.
Didn't just say "less" — diagnosed why it wasn't working (drawing focus from the invitation) and quantified the fix.
Reflection

What AI Fluency & Claude 101 changed about how I work

Before AI Fluency and Claude 101, I prompted the way most people start out—short requests, minimal context, and hoping the first answer would be close enough. The courses changed that. I learned that vague prompts often produce vague responses, so I now spend more time defining the task, providing context, and specifying the constraints before asking AI to solve a problem.

One of the most practical techniques I learned was asking AI to review my prompt before completing the task. Having it point out missing context or unclear instructions has consistently helped me improve my prompts before moving on to the actual work.

The clearest example of this shift is the project behind this portfolio. I created a Claude Project, uploaded my resume, the Claude Corps Fellowship requirements, and supporting documents, then worked through multiple rounds of review and refinement to develop my resume, application responses, and this portfolio. Rather than treating AI as a one-time question-and-answer tool, I used the project as an evolving workspace where each iteration built on the previous one.

Building this portfolio wasn't just the outcome of what I learned—it was the process through which I applied it.
