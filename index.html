Case Study

From one market question to a repeatable decision-support workflow

A one-time options-trading question became a structured daily research process through repeated cycles of constraint-setting, correction, and evaluation.

Transferable pattern:
Define rules before reviewing recommendations, challenge attractive outputs, correct faulty assumptions, and automate only after the process is reliable.
Goal

Create a consistent way to evaluate possible options trades without relying on vague recommendations.

Challenge

Early answers varied because the request did not yet contain a complete decision framework.

Approach

Added price, volatility, delta, risk, and timing constraints, then reviewed each answer against them.

Result

A repeatable report format that could be refined, scheduled, and improved as new gaps appeared.

“My bad. I do NOT want to trade in stocks that are more than $80.”

This correction mattered because I did not simply accept a polished result. I compared it with my actual intent, found the contradiction in my own instruction, and repaired the specification before continuing.

Prompt Evolution

Moving from a request to a usable brief

A better prompt is not necessarily longer. It is clearer about the purpose, audience, constraints, and standard for a good result.

Initial request
“Help improve my resume.”

The task is clear at a high level, but the desired role, selection process, tone, and authenticity constraints are missing.

→
Improved brief
“Tailor my resume for this fellowship. Use relevant keywords, keep the tone professional and strong, preserve only experience I can honestly support, and make the summary easy for both an ATS and a human recruiter to understand.”

The improved version gives the AI a target, guardrails, and a way to judge success.

Reflection

What I learned by applying AI fluency

Before AI Fluency and Claude 101, I often began with a short request and hoped the first answer would be close enough. I now spend more time defining the task, sharing relevant context, and identifying constraints before asking AI to help.

One practical habit that changed my work is asking AI to critique my prompt before completing the task. That helps surface missing information and unclear instructions early, while keeping me responsible for evaluating the final result.

I used that approach throughout my Claude Corps application. In a Claude Project, I brought together my resume, fellowship requirements, and supporting materials, then worked through multiple rounds of review and refinement. The workspace became a place to think, test, correct, and improve—not a one-time question-and-answer session.

Building this portfolio wasn't just the outcome of what I learned—it was the process through which I applied it.
